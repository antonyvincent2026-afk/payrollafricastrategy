PayFlow Africa · User Research Guide

Who to talk to first —
find the hair-on-fire segment

Not all SMEs feel payroll pain equally. Your job is to find the ones whose house is already burning — businesses where the current process is costing real money, causing real stress, and where they'd pay for a fix today. Here's exactly who they are.

🔥
Why now is the perfect time to do these interviews: Botswana passed a sweeping new Employment and Labour Relations Bill in June 2025 — consolidating all employment laws and adding new compliance obligations. BURS also updated tax bands in April 2025. Any SME using last year's spreadsheet is already non-compliant. The compliance anxiety is at a peak. Go now.
The filter logic
3 variables that predict "hair on fire" payroll pain
👥
10–80 employees
Too big for cash-in-hand. Too small to afford an accountant. This is where manual payroll is a real monthly nightmare.
📋
Formal sector, salaried
Businesses with PAYE obligations to BURS. If they're not filing ITW-7 monthly, they're accumulating fines they don't know about.
📅
Monthly recurring payroll
The pain must happen every single month. Not seasonal. Not one-off. The frustration compounds each cycle.
Ranked segments — most to least "on fire"
1
Private schools & tutoring centres
15–80 teachers + admin staff · Gaborone, Francistown
🔥 Hair on fire ▾
Payroll pain intensity
Why they hurt most
Teachers have complex pay: basic salary + housing allowance + subject supplements + overtime for exams. Different contracts for part-time vs full-time. Every month is a manual nightmare.
The compliance trap
BURS requires PAYE on all staff including part-timers. Most private schools don't file correctly for contract teachers. The new 2025 Employment Bill adds leave tracking requirements they're not ready for.
Who to call
School owner/director OR the school bursar (finance admin). They're the ones personally doing payroll on Excel every month-end.
Willingness to pay
High. They already pay accountants BWP 500–2,000/month to file returns. PayFlow at BWP 300–800/month is cheaper and more comprehensive.
How to find them in Botswana
Google Maps"Private school Gaborone" — call the ones with 10+ Google reviews (established, have real staff)
Facebook Groups"Botswana Business Network" — private school owners post there regularly
Your networkAsk family: which private school did you attend? Go talk to their admin office directly
Botswana Private Schools Assoc.Member list is semi-public — contact secretary for intro
Questions to ask the school bursar or owner
  • Walk me through exactly how you run payroll every month — what does that process look like?
  • What's the hardest part of paying staff every month? What takes the most time?
  • How do you calculate PAYE for teachers who work part-time or on contract?
  • When BURS changes tax rates, how do you find out and update your system?
  • How much time does it take you personally to process one month of payroll?
  • What happens if you make a mistake — have you ever had a problem with BURS?
2
Private clinics & medical practices
10–50 staff · Doctors, nurses, receptionists, cleaners
🔥 Hair on fire ▾
Payroll pain intensity
Why they hurt most
Mixed workforce: salaried doctors, hourly nurses, shift-based cleaners. Overtime is constant. Night allowances. The doctor-owner is too busy seeing patients to do payroll properly.
The compliance trap
Healthcare workers' overtime rules are strict under Botswana's Employment Act. The new 2025 Bill tightens enforcement. Most private clinics are exposed to back-pay liability they don't know about.
Who to call
The clinic owner (usually the lead doctor) OR a practice manager if they have one. In smaller clinics, the doctor literally does payroll themselves on a weekend.
Willingness to pay
Very high. Doctors earn well and value their time. If you can save them 3 hours/month, BWP 500/month is nothing. They're also very worried about BURS audits.
How to find them
Google Maps"Private clinic Gaborone" or "Medical practice Botswana" — call 10 of them
Yellow Pages BWBotswana Yellow Pages still has active healthcare listings
Walk inGo to the clinic, ask to speak to the practice manager about an admin question
Healthcare WhatsApp groupsBotswana medical professional groups — ask your friend's family connections
Questions to ask the clinic owner or practice manager
  • Tell me how you currently manage paying your staff every month — take me through the full process.
  • How do you track overtime and night shift allowances for nurses?
  • What's the most stressful part of month-end for you personally?
  • Have you ever had a BURS inquiry or compliance issue? How did that go?
  • Do you use a payroll bureau or accountant? What do they charge you?
  • If you could eliminate one admin task in your practice, what would it be?
3
Security & cleaning companies
20–150 guards/cleaners · Shift-based, minimum wage workers
⚡ Very high pain ▾
Payroll pain intensity
Why they hurt a lot
High headcount, low margin, complex shift patterns. Guards work 12-hour shifts, nights, weekends, public holidays. Every single variation changes the calculation. Errors cause staff to strike.
The compliance trap
Security is specifically named in Botswana's minimum wage regulations. The new Employment Bill's overtime provisions hit this sector hard. Many guards are owed back-pay for holiday work.
Who to call
The operations manager or owner. In smaller security firms (20–60 guards), the owner runs payroll. In medium ones (60–150), there's a dedicated admin person drowning in it.
Willingness to pay
Medium-high. Margins are thin but the pain is extreme. They pay accountants already. Frame it as risk reduction (BURS penalties, labour disputes) not just convenience.
How to find them
Google"Security company Gaborone Botswana" — dozens of local firms
Shopping mallsEvery mall in Gaborone uses a local security firm. Ask the guard who their employer is.
BOCCIM directoryBotswana Chamber of Commerce has a business directory
Cold callCall 20 security firms, say you're a student researching payroll challenges in Botswana
Questions to ask the owner/ops manager
  • How many guards do you currently employ, and how do you pay them each month?
  • How do you track who worked what shifts — especially night shifts and public holidays?
  • What's the hardest calculation you have to do every month when running payroll?
  • Have you ever had guards complain that their pay was wrong? What happened?
  • How much time does your team spend on payroll processing every month-end?
  • How do you make sure you're compliant with BURS on PAYE for all staff?
4
Growing retail shops & supermarkets
10–40 staff · Family-owned, expanding beyond one location
⚡ High pain ▾
Payroll pain intensity
Why they hurt
As family shops grow from 3 staff to 15+, the informal cash-in-hand system breaks. They suddenly need PAYE, proper contracts, leave tracking. Most don't know where to start.
The compliance trap
Many growing shops have never registered with BURS for PAYE. They're technically non-compliant and accumulating penalties. The new Employment Bill's enforcement focus makes this urgent.
Who to call
The shop owner (often a family patriarch/matriarch). They are personally handling wages. Go to the shop, ask to speak to the owner about a business question.
Willingness to pay
Medium. Price-sensitive. But the fear of BURS penalties and the 2025 compliance bill creates urgency. Lead with risk, not features.
How to find them
Walk aroundAny medium-sized independent shop in Gaborone's Game City, Rail Park Mall areas
Family networkAsk your friend's family: which shops do you use? Who owns them?
Facebook Marketplace BWMany growing shops advertise there — message them
Botswana Business Directorygovt.bw has a business registry you can search
Questions to ask the shop owner
  • How many people do you employ currently, and how do you pay them each month?
  • When did you start having to deal with PAYE and tax deductions — how did you figure that out?
  • What does your payroll process look like today — how long does it take you?
  • Have you ever had a problem with staff saying they were paid the wrong amount?
  • If you opened a second shop, how would you manage payroll for two locations?
  • What's the most time-consuming admin task in running this business?
5
Tech startups & professional services
5–25 staff · Accountants, lawyers, consultants, agencies
📊 Good signal ▾
Payroll pain intensity
Why they're useful — but not #1
Easy to reach, English-speaking, tech-literate — but their pain is lower because they often already use Excel competently or have a part-time accountant. Good for product feedback, not the most desperate users.
When to talk to them
After you've validated the core pain with schools, clinics, and security firms. Startups will give you articulate feedback. Use them to test your prototype, not to discover the problem.
Their specific pain
Consultants who work across countries (Botswana + South Africa or Zimbabwe) have multi-currency, multi-tax payroll that Excel can't handle. That's a real acute pain for them specifically.
Who to call
The founder/managing partner. Find them through Botswana Innovation Hub, BOCCIM young entrepreneurs chapter, or LinkedIn — Botswana's professional network is small and reachable.
Questions to ask startup founders
  • How do you currently run payroll — do you have an in-house person or outsource it?
  • What's the most annoying part of doing payroll every month?
  • Have you ever paid someone across two countries? How did you handle that?
  • What tools do you currently use — Excel, QuickBooks, something else?
  • What would make you switch to a new payroll tool?
🔥
The verdict: start with private schools
Schools are your perfect first segment. They have 15–80 salaried staff with complex pay structures. The owner/bursar does payroll personally on Excel every month. BURS compliance is a real stress — the new 2025 Employment Bill added new obligations they're not ready for. They already pay accountants BWP 500–2,000/month, so price is not the objection. They're reachable (call them directly). They're concentrated in Gaborone. And your friend almost certainly knows someone who works at or attended a private school in Botswana — that's your warm intro.
Your interview plan — this week
20 interviews in 2 weeks — here's the exact playbook
1
Get 5 warm intros from family/friends network
Your friend's family in Botswana knows someone who runs a private school, clinic, or small business. Ask specifically: "Do you know anyone who owns a business with 10+ employees?" That's your first 5 interviews.
2
Cold call 15 private schools in Gaborone
Google "private school Gaborone" → pick 15 → call and say: "Hi, I'm a Botswana student studying at Northwestern University in the US. I'm researching how businesses manage payroll in Botswana. Could I speak with whoever handles your school's payroll for 20 minutes? I just want to understand the process — I'm not selling anything."
3
Run the interview — listen, don't pitch
Use the questions from the segment cards above. Record with permission or take notes. The goal is to hear them describe pain in their own words — phrases you can use directly in your product copy and pitch deck later.
4
After 20 interviews: look for the pattern
Write down every complaint you heard. Which phrases came up in 15 of the 20 interviews? That's your product's headline. Which problems came up in 3? That's a nice-to-have. Build what they kept repeating.
5
Create a WhatsApp group with the most engaged 5–8
At the end of the interview, if they were engaged, say: "I'm building a tool to solve exactly this. Would you be willing to be one of the first people to see it and give feedback? I'll add you to a small group." These become your design partners and first paying customers.
What not to say in the interview
These questions will give you useless answers — avoid them
❌
"Would you use our product?"
Everyone says yes to be polite. Means nothing. Instead ask how they do it today and listen for pain.
❌
"What features would you want?"
Users design for what they know, not what's possible. They'll say "export to Excel" when what they need is to never use Excel again.
❌
"I'm building a payroll app—"
The moment you pitch, they stop being honest. They tell you what they think you want to hear. Ask about their life, not about your product.
